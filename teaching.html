Teaching

I study some of the most distant and luminous quasars—supermassive black holes actively accreting material in the early universe. I lead multi-institutional projects involving near-infrared spectroscopic observations of quasars at high redshifts (z = 1.5 - 3.5) with the Gemini-North Telescope. I derive black hole masses, accretion rates, and systemic redshifts using rest-frame optical emission lines, improving the accuracy of UV-based quasar property estimates that are widely used in extragalactic surveys, such as JWST and DESI.

My work also involves understanding Broad Absorption Line (BAL) quasars that show strong outflows as absorption features in their UV spectra. I have conducted multiwavelength analysis of luminous BAL sources to explore how dust reddening, orientation, and outflows shape their observed properties.

These studies contribute to our broader understanding of supermassive black hole growth, quasar winds, and feedback in the early universe.
